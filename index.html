What is Ecological Jurisprudence?

Ecological jurisprudence is a broad field of legal theory and practice that recognises the rights of nature and the intrinsic value of ecosystems. It challenges human-centred legal frameworks by placing ecological health at the centre of law.

Drawing on Earth jurisprudence, indigenous legal traditions, and rights-of-nature scholarship, ecological jurisprudence seeks to transform how legal systems relate to the living world.

What do we include?

We track legal initiatives from around the world that formally recognise the rights, interests, or legal personhood of nature — whether through constitutions, legislation, court cases, policies, or civil society instruments.

We include both formal law (state-enacted) and soft law (civil society declarations, tribunals, and governance frameworks) that meaningfully advance ecological jurisprudence.

What types of legal initiatives are included?

Initiatives span constitutions, national and subnational legislation, court cases, executive policies, formal declarations, indigenous law, civil society declarations and tribunals, private sector governance frameworks, and position statements.

Each initiative is categorised by its primary legal form and mapped to a region and one or more nature types.

What types of eco jurisprudence are included?

We include initiatives protecting all nature types: water bodies (rivers, lakes, oceans, wetlands), forests and plants, animals and species, land-based ecosystems (mountains, grasslands, deserts), climate and atmospheric systems, and initiatives granting rights to nature as a whole.

Initiatives covering multiple nature types or entire ecosystems are categorised accordingly.

How we gather this data

Data is gathered through a combination of continuous monitoring of legal journals, news sources, government publications, and civil society reports. Our team reviews and verifies each initiative before it is added to the tracker.

We also rely on a global network of partners and contributors who notify us of new or updated initiatives in their regions.

How we track: a participatory model

The EJM operates on a participatory model: anyone can submit a new initiative or correction through our data request form. Submissions are reviewed by our editorial team before being published.

Regional representatives help maintain accuracy within their areas of expertise, ensuring the tracker reflects ground-level developments.

To meet our criteria, an initiative must:

Have a formal legal basis or documented civil society status. Explicitly reference the rights, interests, or legal standing of nature or a specific ecosystem. Be verifiable through primary or credible secondary sources. Have been initiated or formally recognised from 2006 onwards (or be a documented precursor of contemporary movements).
